Abstract

This work examines the theoretical consistency and implications of a unified natural unit system in which both the fine-structure constant \(α_{\text{EM}}= 1\) and the T0 model parameter \(β_{\text{T}}^{\text{nat}} = 1\) are set. Through detailed mathematical analyses, dimensional considerations, and an investigation of fundamental interactions, it is demonstrated that this dual simplification leads to a coherent and elegant theoretical framework. The characteristic length scales of the model are identified as dimensionless ratios to the Planck length, revealing a deeper connection between electrodynamics, time-mass duality, and quantum gravitation. This unified perspective offers new insights for the T0 model and could pave the way toward a more fundamental unification theory.

1 Introduction

The simplification of physical theories through the choice of appropriate unit systems has a long tradition in theoretical physics. In special relativity, the speed of light is set to \(c = 1\), in quantum mechanics the Planck constant is set to \(\hbar = 1\), and in quantum gravitation the gravitational constant is set to \(G = 1\). These simplifications are not merely mathematical but reveal fundamental structures of physics.

In previous works , two additional simplifications were independently explored: setting the fine-structure constant \(α_{\text{EM}}= 1\) and setting the T0 model parameter \(β_{\text{T}}^{\text{nat}} = 1\). This work takes a step further and systematically examines the consistency and implications of a unified unit system in which both parameters are simultaneously set to 1.

Such a unification is nontrivial, as \(α_{\text{EM}}\) characterizes the electromagnetic interaction, while \(β_{\text{T}}\) relates to the coupling between the intrinsic time field \(T(x)\) and other fields in the T0 model (see ). The compatibility of both simplifications could provide deeper insights into the fundamental structure of physics and potentially point toward a unification of electrodynamics and gravitation within the T0 model framework as outlined in .

2 Foundations of the Unified Unit System

2.1 The Fine-Structure Constant \(α_{\text{EM}}\) and Its Setting to 1

The fine-structure constant \(α_{\text{EM}}\) is defined as:

\[α_{\text{EM}}= (e^2)/(4πε_0 \hbar c) ≈ (1)/(137.036)\](1)

When setting \(α_{\text{EM}}= 1\), it follows:

\[e = \sqrt{4πε_0 \hbar c}\](2)

This implies that the elementary charge becomes a dimensionless quantity, precisely defined by fundamental constants. In a system with \(\hbar = c = 1\), this simplifies to:

\[e = \sqrt{4πε_0}\](3)

The consequence is that electric charges become dimensionless, and all electromagnetic quantities can be reduced to energy, as detailed in .

2.2 The Parameter \(β_{\text{T}}\) in the T0 Model and Its Setting to 1

In the T0 model, the parameter \(β_{\text{T}}\) characterizes the coupling between the intrinsic time field \(T(x)\) and other fields. The standard formulation in natural units is:

\[β_{\text{T}}^{\text{nat}} = (λ_h^2 v^2)/(16π^3 m_h^2 ξ)\](4)

where \(λ_h\) is the Higgs self-coupling, \(v\) is the Higgs vacuum expectation value, \(m_h\) is the Higgs mass, and \(ξ\) is a dimensionless parameter that defines the characteristic length scale of the model as \(r_0 = ξ · l_P\), with \(l_P\) being the Planck length (see ).

In SI units, this parameter has the approximate value \(β_{\text{T}}^{\text{SI}} ≈ 0.008\), which can be derived from the natural units value through the relation:

\[β_{\text{T}}^{\text{SI}} = β_{\text{T}}^{\text{nat}} · \frac{ξ · l_{P,\text{SI}}}{r_{0,\text{SI}}}\](5)

Setting \(β_{\text{T}}^{\text{nat}} = 1\) in natural units implies:

\[ξ = (λ_h^2 v^2)/(16π^3 m_h^2) ≈ 1.33 × 10^{-4}\](6)

This fixes the characteristic length scale to \(r_0 ≈ 1.33 × 10^{-4} · l_P\), approximately \(1/7519\) of the Planck length. The complete derivation of this relationship is provided in .

3 Analysis of the Consistency of \(α_{\text{EM}}= 1\) and \(β_{\text{T}}^{\text{nat}} = 1\)

3.1 Dimensional Analysis of the \(β_{\text{T}}\)-Formula

To verify the consistency of \(α_{\text{EM}}= 1\) and \(β_{\text{T}}^{\text{nat}} = 1\), we perform a detailed dimensional analysis of the \(β_{\text{T}}\)-formula:

\[β_{\text{T}}^{\text{nat}} = (λ_h^2 v^2)/(16π^3 m_h^2 ξ)\](7)

Let’s analyze the dimensions of each term:

1. \(λ_h\) (Higgs self-coupling): dimensionless 2. \(v\) (Higgs vacuum expectation value): \([E]\) (energy) 3. \(m_h\) (Higgs mass): \([E]\) (energy) 4. \(ξ\) (dimensionless parameter): dimensionless

Substituting these dimensions into the \(β_{\text{T}}\)-formula:

\[\begin{aligned} β_{\text{T}}^{\text{nat}} &= [1] · [E]^2 / ([1] · [E]^2 · [1]) \\ &= [E]^2 / [E]^2 \\ &= [1] \end{aligned}\](8)

This dimensional analysis confirms that \(β_{\text{T}}^{\text{nat}}\) is indeed dimensionless, as required for a fundamental parameter. Setting \(α_{\text{EM}}= 1\) does not affect this dimensional homogeneity, since \(α_{\text{EM}}\) itself is dimensionless and does not explicitly appear in the \(β_{\text{T}}\)-formula (see also ).

3.2 Relationship Between \(r_0\) and the Planck Length with \(α_{\text{EM}}= 1\)

With \(β_{\text{T}}^{\text{nat}} = 1\), we have derived the relationship \(r_0 = ξ · l_P\) with \(ξ = (λ_h^2 v^2)/(16π^3 m_h^2)\). The question now is whether this relationship remains consistent when \(α_{\text{EM}}= 1\).

Setting \(α_{\text{EM}}= 1\) could alter the values of the electroweak parameters \(λ_h\), \(v\), and \(m_h\), but their fundamental relationships should persist. In particular, the Standard Model relation holds:

\[m_h^2 = 2λ_h v^2\](9)

Substituting this into the formula for \(ξ\):

\[\begin{aligned} ξ &= (λ_h^2 v^2)/(16π^3 · 2λ_h v^2) \\ &= (λ_h)/(32π^3) \end{aligned}\](10)

With \(λ_h ≈ 0.13\), this yields:

\[\begin{aligned} ξ &≈ (0.13)/(32π^3) \\ &≈ (0.13)/(990) \\ &≈ 1.31 × 10^{-4} \end{aligned}\](11)

This value is nearly identical to the previously calculated \(ξ ≈ 1.33 × 10^{-4}\), suggesting that the relationship between \(r_0\) and the Planck length is robust and independent of \(α_{\text{EM}}\) (see ).

3.3 Relationship Between Electroweak Parameters and \(α_{\text{EM}}\)

In the Standard Model, there are several relationships between the electroweak parameters and the fine-structure constant \(α_{\text{EM}}\). For example, fermion masses are given by the Yukawa couplings \(y_f\) and the Higgs vacuum expectation value \(v\):

\[m_f = y_f · v\](12)

The Yukawa couplings themselves are linked to the electroweak coupling and thus indirectly to \(α_{\text{EM}}\). Setting \(α_{\text{EM}}= 1\) would increase the strength of the electromagnetic interaction, potentially affecting the Yukawa couplings as well. This connection is explored in detail in .

4.3 Complete Intrinsic Lagrangian Density

The intrinsic Lagrangian density of the time field, which is fundamental to the T0 model’s formulation, is given by:

\[\mathcal{L}_{\text{intrinsic}} = (1)/(2) ∂_μ T(x)∂^μ T(x)- (1)/(2)T(x)^2 - (ρ)/(T(x))\](16)

where the first term represents the kinetic energy of the time field, the second term represents its self-interaction potential, and the third term describes the coupling between the time field and matter with matter density \(ρ\) having dimension \([E^2]\) in natural units.

Applying the Euler-Lagrange equations to this Lagrangian density:

\[∂_μ \left( \frac{∂ \mathcal{L}}{∂(∂_μ T(x))} \right) - \frac{∂ \mathcal{L}}{∂ T(x)} = 0\](17)

yields the field equation for the time field:

\[∇^2 T(x)- (∂^2 T(x))/(∂ t^2) = -T(x)- (ρ)/(T(x)^2)\](18)

In regions with significant matter density, the dominant term is \((ρ)/(T(x)^2)\), and for static mass distributions, the time derivative vanishes, simplifying the equation to:

\[∇^2 T(x)≈ -(ρ)/(T(x)^2) = -κ ρ(\vec{x}) T(x)^2\](19)

where \(κ\) has dimension \([E]\) in natural units and equals unity when \(β_{\text{T}}^{\text{nat}} = 1\).

4.4 Field Equation for the Intrinsic Time Field

The dynamics of the intrinsic time field \(T(x)\) are governed by the field equation:

\[∇^2 T(x)= -κ ρ(\vec{x}) T(x)^2\](20)

This equation, derived from the Lagrangian density in section \(Intrinsic Time Field in Lagrangian Formulation\), forms the foundation for emergent gravitation in the T0 model. The parameter \(κ\) with dimension \([E]\) in natural units equals unity when \(β_{\text{T}}^{\text{nat}} = 1\).

For a point mass \(M\), this equation yields the solution:

\[T(x)(r) = T_0\left(1 - (M)/(r)\right)\](21)

which leads directly to the modified gravitational potential discussed in section \(T0 Model Equations\).

This work has investigated the theoretical consistency and implications of a unified natural unit system in which both the fine-structure constant \(α_{\text{EM}}= 1\) and the T0 model parameter \(β_{\text{T}}^{\text{nat}} = 1\) are set. The main findings are:

  1. Setting \(α_{\text{EM}}= 1\) and \(β_{\text{T}}^{\text{nat}} = 1\) simultaneously is mathematically consistent and leads to an elegant theoretical framework where energy is the fundamental unit.

  2. The characteristic length scale \(r_0\) of the T0 model can be interpreted as a specific ratio to the Planck length: \(r_0 ≈ 1.33 × 10^{-4} · l_P\), independent of the value of \(α_{\text{EM}}\).

  3. The field equations for both electrodynamics and T0 dynamics take particularly simple forms in this unified unit system, suggesting a deeper connection between the two interactions.

  4. The unified model provides specific predictions for cosmological observations, particularly regarding wavelength-dependent redshift and cosmic temperature evolution (see ).

The unification presented here opens new perspectives for the T0 model and could lead to a deeper unification theory that describes electrodynamics and emergent gravitation within a coherent framework. Future research should focus on the precise mathematical formulation of this unification and experimental tests to verify the model’s validity.
