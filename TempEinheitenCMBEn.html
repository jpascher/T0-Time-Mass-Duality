3 Adjustment of the Temperature Unit with \(α_{\text{W}}= 1\)

The consistent application of the principle of maximum simplification in natural unit systems has profound implications for the interpretation and scaling of thermodynamic quantities. In particular, the relationship between temperature and energy must be reconsidered. Planck’s radiation formula, which describes the spectral energy density of blackbody radiation:

\[u(ν, T) = (2π h ν^3)/(c^2) · (1)/(e^{h ν / k_B T) - 1}\](1)

leads to Wien’s displacement law, which relates the frequency of the radiation maximum to temperature:

\[ν_{\text{max}} = α_{\text{W}}· (k_B T)/(h)\](2)

where \(α_{\text{W}}≈ 2.821439\) is a numerically determined constant. If, in addition to \(k_B = 1\), \(h = 2π\) (since \(\hbar = 1\)), \(c = 1\), we also set \(α_{\text{W}}= 1\), a direct proportionality between the frequency of the radiation maximum and temperature results:

\[ν_{\text{max}} = (T)/(2π)\](3)

To make this relationship consistent, an adjustment of the temperature unit is required. Kelvin would be inappropriate as a base unit, as temperature would then have to be measured and scaled directly in energy units to match the frequency of the radiation maximum. This adjustment is analogous to the treatment of space and time in relativity theory, where with \(c = 1\), both can be measured in length units. Setting \(α_{\text{W}}= 1\) is thus a consistent extension of the principle of maximum simplification, but requires a redefinition of the temperature unit. In the context of the T0 model , where mass varies with the intrinsic time field \(T(x)\), this redefinition could align with the model’s framework, although temperatures are typically given in Kelvin for practical reasons .

4 Detailed Adjustment of the Temperature Unit with \(α_{\text{W}}= 1\)

The document on the fine-structure constant Natural Units with Fine-structure Constant \(α = 1\) proposes an approach that can also be applied to Wien’s constant: In natural units with \(k_B = 1\), \(h = 2π\) (since \(\hbar = 1\)), \(c = 1\), and additionally \(α_{\text{W}}= 1\), temperature directly corresponds to the frequency of the radiation maximum (\(ν_{\text{max}} = T\)), when the temperature unit is scaled accordingly. Let’s derive this relationship systematically:

4.1 Standard Formula

Wien’s displacement law in SI units is:

\[ν_{\text{max}} = α_{\text{W}}· (k_B T)/(h), \quad α_{\text{W}}≈ 2.821439,\](4)
where \(α_{\text{W}}\) is numerically determined by maximizing the Planck distribution by solving the equation \(3 (e^x - 1) = x e^x\).

4.2 Natural Units

With \(k_B = 1\), \(h = 2π\) (since \(\hbar = 1\)), \(c = 1\):

\[ν_{\text{max}} = α_{\text{W}}· (T)/(2π),\](5)
\[ν_{\text{max}} = (2.821439)/(2π) T ≈ 0.449 T.\](6)

In natural units, \(α_{\text{W}}≈ 2.821439\) persists as it is a mathematical constant independent of \(h\), \(c\), or \(k_B\). It represents an intrinsic feature of blackbody radiation, similar to how the fine-structure constant \(α_{\text{EM}}\) is an intrinsic feature of electromagnetic interaction.

4.3 Setting \(α_{\text{W}}= 1\)

If we set \(α_{\text{W}}= 1\):

\[ν_{\text{max}} = (T)/(2π),\](7)
or, if we absorb the \(2π\) factors by appropriate scaling of temperature:
\[T_{\text{scaled}} = 2π T,\](8)
so that:
\[ν_{\text{max}} = T_{\text{scaled}}.\](9)

6.2 Temperature Calculation and Conversion Between Unit Systems

6.2.0.1 Basic Premise

If \(β_{\text{T}}^{\text{SI}} = 0.008\) and \(β_{\text{T}}^{\text{nat}} = 1\) are equivalent representations of the same physical parameter in different unit systems, both calculations must lead to the same physical result after appropriate conversion.

6.2.0.2 Calculation in the SI System

Starting from the standard background temperature and applying the temperature-redshift relationship of the T0 model:

\[\begin{aligned} T(1101) &= 2.725 \, \text{K} × 1101 × (1 + 0.008 × \ln(1101)) \\ &= 2.725 \, \text{K} × 1101 × 1.056 \\ &= 3.198 \, \text{K} \end{aligned}\](17)

6.2.0.3 Conversion to Frequency

Converting this temperature to frequency with Wien’s displacement law with \(α_{\text{W}}^{\text{SI}} ≈ 2.821\):

\[\begin{aligned} ν_{\text{max}} &= α_{\text{W}}^{\text{SI}} · (k_B T)/(h) \\ &= 2.821 · \frac{1.381 × 10^{-23} × 3.198}{6.626 × 10^{-34}} \\ &≈ 3 × 10^{14} \, \text{Hz} \end{aligned}\](18)

6.2.0.4 Calculation in the Natural Unit System

Converting the base temperature to natural units with \(α_{\text{W}}= 1\):

\[\begin{aligned} T_0^{\text{nat}} &= T_0^{\text{SI}} · (k_B)/(h) · \frac{α_{\text{W}}^{\text{SI}}}{α_{\text{W}}^{\text{nat}}} \\ &= 2.725 \, \text{K} · \frac{1.381 × 10^{-23}}{2π · 1.055 × 10^{-34}} · (2.821)/(1) \\ &≈ 7.14 × 10^{10} \, \text{Hz} \end{aligned}\](19)

6.2.0.5 Temperature Calculation in Natural Units

In the natural unit system with \(β_{\text{T}}^{\text{nat}} = 1\), we calculate the temperature at redshift z = 1101:

\[\begin{aligned} T(1101)^{\text{nat}} &= T_0^{\text{nat}} × 1101 × (1 + \ln(1101)) \\ &= 7.14 × 10^{10} \, \text{Hz} × 1101 × (1 + 7.0) \\ &= 7.14 × 10^{10} \, \text{Hz} × 1101 × 8.0 \\ &≈ 6.29 × 10^{14} \, \text{Hz} \end{aligned}\](20)

6.2.0.6 Frequency Normalization

The calculated frequency must be normalized to account for the difference between \(α_{\text{W}}^{\text{SI}} = 2.821\) and \(α_{\text{W}}^{\text{nat}} = 1\):

\[\begin{aligned} ν_{\text{max}}^{\text{normalized}} &= \frac{ν_{\text{max}}^{\text{nat}}}{α_{\text{W}}^{\text{SI}}} × α_{\text{W}}^{\text{nat}} \\ &= \frac{6.29 × 10^{14} \, \text{Hz}}{2.821} × 1 \\ &≈ 2.23 × 10^{14} \, \text{Hz} \end{aligned}\](21)

6.2.0.7 Conversion Back to SI Temperature

Converting this frequency back to temperature in the SI system:

\[\begin{aligned} T_{\text{final}} &= \frac{h · ν_{\text{max}}^{\text{normalized}}}{k_B · α_{\text{W}}^{\text{SI}}} \\ &= \frac{6.626 × 10^{-34} × 2.23 × 10^{14}}{1.381 × 10^{-23} × 2.821} \\ &≈ 4.36 \, \text{K} \end{aligned}\](22)

6.3.0.3 Reconciliation and Derivation of the Correction Factor

The factor of 1.6 arises from the different physical interpretations of redshift:

  1. Standard Model: Interprets redshift as purely kinematic, affecting temperature as \(T ∝ (1+z)\)

  2. T0 Model: Interprets redshift as energy loss, affecting temperature as \(T ∝ (1+z)(1+\ln(1+z))\)

For a redshift of \(z = 1101\) (CMB), the correction factor is:

\[((1+z)(1+\ln(1+z)))/((1+z)) = (1+\ln(1+z)) ≈ 8.0\](28)

When normalized by the ratio of \(α_{\text{W}}\) values (\(2.821/1\)) and considering the conversion between temperature and frequency, we obtain the final factor of 1.6.
