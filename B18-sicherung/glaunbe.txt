**Part 1 of 4 — The language trap**

Much of this confusion is built into language itself.

In English, 'belief' and 'faith' are two different words. Belief is a propositional attitude — holding something to be true. Faith carries connotations of trust, commitment, resistance to revision. Philosophers use them differently. Most scientists conflate them.

In German the situation is worse: both concepts collapse into a single word — 'Glaube'. So when a German-speaking physicist says 'Ich glaube nicht, ich weiß' (I don't believe, I know), the word he is rejecting carries the full weight of religious faith, not just propositional belief. The rejection feels more radical than it is — and more justified than it is.

This is not a minor semantic point. It means the entire debate runs differently depending on which language you are having it in. In English you can carefully distinguish belief from faith. In German you have to fight the language itself to make the same distinction.

The scientist is not just confused about epistemology. He is also, in part, a victim of his own vocabulary.

**Part 2 of 4 — The invisible zoo**

Hebrews 11:1 defines faith as the conviction of things not seen.

This is philosophically precise in a way that is rarely acknowledged. Because this is exactly what van Fraassen calls 'acceptance' — and exactly what every physicist does when he treats electrons, quarks, dark energy, or the wave function as real. None of these have ever been directly observed. All are inferred from their effects.

The physicist who says 'I don't believe anything' believes in an entire zoo of invisible entities, accessible only through their consequences. The epistemological structure is identical to what Hebrews 11:1 describes. Only the vocabulary differs.

Van Fraassen himself — one of the most cited philosophers of science — does not deny this. He says: I believe many things. I believe I had a father. I believe I wrote a book. The question is not whether scientists have beliefs. It is which beliefs they are willing to examine.

**Part 3 of 4 — The unexamined axioms**

The deeper problem is not that scientists have beliefs. It is that certain beliefs were elevated to axioms so early, and so quietly, that they became invisible.

The belief that space has no physical properties. The belief that quantum randomness is ontological rather than epistemic. The belief that a mathematically consistent description constitutes a physical explanation.

None of these were conclusions from evidence. They were choices — made at a particular moment in history, for particular reasons, and then forgotten as choices. They became the water the fish swims in.

This is precisely what makes them dangerous. A belief you know you hold can be examined, updated, replaced. A belief you do not know you hold shapes everything you see without ever appearing as a target for revision.

Epistemic humility does not mean having no beliefs. It means knowing which ones are doing the most work — and being willing to look at them.

---

**Part 4 of 4 — The more honest approach**

The solution is not to pretend you have no beliefs. It is to state them explicitly and hold them as what they are — working assumptions, not eternal truths.

In my own work on T0 theory, the foundational assumption is stated openly: that a single geometric parameter ξ = 4/30000 underlies all fundamental constants. This cannot be measured directly. It is inferred from its consequences — lepton masses, the fine-structure constant, the anomalous magnetic moment. That is, epistemically, exactly the Hebrews 11:1 mode of knowing.

I do not think this makes T0 less scientific. I think it makes it more honest than a framework that inserts 25+ parameters by hand and calls that an explanation.

The scientist who says 'I believe electrons exist, and I am willing to revise that belief if the evidence demands it' — is not being unscientific. He is modeling exactly the epistemic virtue this video calls for.

That statement is not a confession of irrationality. It is a description of how knowledge actually works.
