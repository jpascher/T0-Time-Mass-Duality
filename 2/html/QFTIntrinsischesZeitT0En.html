Abstract

This work presents a systematic quantum field theoretical treatment of the intrinsic time field \(T(x)\) in the T0 model. Starting from classical field theory, a complete quantization is developed, encompassing canonical commutation relations, path integral formalism, and interaction dynamics. Particular attention is given to the integration of the quantized time field with Standard Model fields through modified propagators and extended Feynman rules. The theory satisfies the requirements of unitarity and causality while providing a natural bridge between quantum mechanics and relativity according to the time-mass duality principle. The quantization yields specific experimental predictions, including quantum corrections to wavelength-dependent redshift and modified gravitational wave propagation. This consistent quantum theory of the intrinsic time field addresses open questions in foundational physics and establishes the T0 model as a promising alternative to conventional approaches to quantum gravity and unified theories.

1 Quantum Field Theoretical Treatment of the T0 Model

1.1 Foundations of the T0 Model

The T0 model provides a novel approach to fundamental physics based on the intrinsic time field \(T(x)\) and time-mass duality. In the T0 framework, the intrinsic time field is defined as:

\[T(x) = (\hbar)/(\max(mc^2, ω)) \label{eq:intrinsic_time}\](1)

In natural units (\(\hbar = c = G = k_B = 1\)), this simplifies to:

\[T(x) = (1)/(m) \label{eq:natural_units_time}\](2)

This relationship establishes energy as the fundamental unit, with \(T(x)\) having dimension \([E^{-1}]\). The complete T0 model is built on unified natural units with \(α_{\text{EM}} = β_T = α_W = 1\), as developed in .

1.2 Field Equations and Lagrangian Density

The field equation for the intrinsic time field is:

\[∇^2 T(x) = -κρ(x)T(x)^2 \label{eq:field_equation}\](3)

where \(κ\) has dimension \([E]\) and \(ρ\) has dimension \([E^2]\) in natural units. As demonstrated in , this equation leads to emergent gravitation.

The total Lagrangian density for the T0 model is:

\[\mathcal{L}_{\text{Total}} = \mathcal{L}_{\text{Boson}} + \mathcal{L}_{\text{Fermion}} + \mathcal{L}_{\text{Higgs-T}} + \mathcal{L}_{\text{intrinsic}} \label{eq:total_lagrangian}\](4)

where:

\[\begin{aligned} \mathcal{L}_{\text{intrinsic}} &= (1)/(2)∂_{μ}T(x)∂^{μ}T(x) - (1)/(2)T(x)^2 - (ρ)/(T(x)) \label{eq:intrinsic_lagrangian} \\ \mathcal{L}_{\text{Boson}} &= -(1)/(4)F_{μν}F^{μν} \label{eq:boson_lagrangian} \\ \mathcal{L}_{\text{Fermion}} &= \bar{ψ}iγ^{μ}D_{Tμ}ψ \label{eq:fermion_lagrangian} \\ \mathcal{L}_{\text{Higgs-T}} &= |D_{Tμ}Φ|^2 - V(Φ) \label{eq:higgs_lagrangian} \end{aligned}\](5)

with the T-modified derivatives:

\[\begin{aligned} D_{Tμ}ψ &= T(x)D_{μ}ψ + ψ∂_{μ}T(x) \label{eq:t_modified_derivative} \\ D_{Tμ}Φ &= T(x)(∂_{μ} + igA_{μ})Φ + Φ∂_{μ}T(x) \label{eq:higgs_t_derivative} \end{aligned}\](6)

These expressions maintain complete consistency with the field equations derived in and .

1.3 Canonical Quantization

To quantize the intrinsic time field, we separate it into a classical background and quantum fluctuations:

\[T(x) = T_c(x) + \hat{T}(x) \label{eq:quantum_decomposition}\](7)

The classical part satisfies:

\[∇^2 T_c(x) = -κρ(x)T_c(x)^2 \label{eq:classical_field_equation}\](8)

For the quantum fluctuations, the effective Lagrangian becomes:

\[\mathcal{L}_{\text{quantum}} ≈ (1)/(2)∂_{μ}\hat{T}(x)∂^{μ}\hat{T}(x) - (1)/(2)\hat{T}(x)^2 - (ρ)/(T_c(x)^3)\hat{T}(x)^2 \label{eq:quantum_lagrangian}\](9)

The canonical momentum is:

\[Π(x) = \frac{∂\mathcal{L}}{∂(∂_0 \hat{T})} = ∂_0 \hat{T}(x) \label{eq:canonical_momentum}\](10)

We impose canonical commutation relations:

\[[\hat{T}(\vec{x}, t), Π(\vec{y}, t)] = iδ^3(\vec{x} - \vec{y}) \label{eq:commutation_relation}\](11)
\[[\hat{T}(\vec{x}, t), \hat{T}(\vec{y}, t)] = [Π(\vec{x}, t), Π(\vec{y}, t)] = 0 \label{eq:field_commutators}\](12)

These relations follow directly from the principles of quantum field theory applied to the intrinsic time field, without importing Standard Model assumptions.

1.4 Mode Expansion and Hamiltonian

The effective mass for quantum fluctuations is:

\[m_{\text{eff}}^2(x) = 1 + (2ρ)/(T_c(x)^3) \label{eq:effective_mass}\](13)

In regions of approximately uniform mass density, we can express \(\hat{T}(x)\) using mode expansion:

\[\hat{T}(x) = ∈t (d^3k)/((2π)^3) (1)/(\sqrt{2ω_{\vec{k)}}} \left(a_{\vec{k}} e^{-ik · x} + a_{\vec{k}}^{\dagger} e^{ik · x}\right) \label{eq:mode_expansion}\](14)

where \(ω_{\vec{k}} = \sqrt{\vec{k}^2 + m_{\text{eff}}^2}\) and:

\[[a_{\vec{k}}, a_{\vec{k'}}^{\dagger}] = (2π)^3 δ^3(\vec{k} - \vec{k'}) \label{eq:creation_annihilation}\](15)

The Hamiltonian density is:

\[\mathcal{H} = (1)/(2)Π(x)^2 + (1)/(2)(∇ \hat{T}(x))^2 + (1)/(2)\left(1 + (2ρ)/(T_c(x)^3)\right)\hat{T}(x)^2 \label{eq:hamiltonian_density}\](16)

After normal ordering, the Hamiltonian becomes:

\[H = ∈t (d^3k)/((2π)^3) ω_{\vec{k}} :a_{\vec{k}}^{\dagger}a_{\vec{k}}: + E_0 \label{eq:hamiltonian}\](17)

The vacuum energy \(E_0\) depends on the matter distribution through \(m_{\text{eff}}\), providing a natural mechanism for vacuum energy to adjust to the presence of matter. This is fundamentally different from the Standard Model approach, which leads to the cosmological constant problem. In the T0 model, this issue is naturally addressed through the coupling between the time field and matter distribution, as detailed in .

1.5 Path Integral Formulation

The generating functional for the quantum time field is:

\[Z[J] = ∈t \mathcal{D}T \exp\left(i∈t d^4x (\mathcal{L}_{\text{quantum}} + J(x)\hat{T}(x))\right) \label{eq:generating_functional}\](18)

which can be evaluated as:

\[Z[J] = \exp\left(-(i)/(2)∈t d^4x d^4y J(x)Δ_F(x-y)J(y)\right) \label{eq:evaluated_functional}\](19)

where \(Δ_F(x-y)\) is the Feynman propagator:

\[Δ_F(x-y) = ∈t (d^4k)/((2π)^4) (i)/(k^2 - m_{\text{eff)}^2 + iε} e^{-ik · (x-y)} \label{eq:feynman_propagator}\](20)

This propagator includes the effective mass \(m_{\text{eff}}\) which contains the matter coupling, a feature unique to the T0 model not found in Standard Model approaches.
