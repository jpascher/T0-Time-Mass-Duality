2 Introduction

Natural units are unit systems where fundamental physical constants are set to unity to simplify calculations and reveal the underlying mathematical structure of physical laws. The most well-known systems are **Planck units** (for gravitation and quantum physics) and **atomic units** (for quantum chemistry).

This document establishes the complete framework for the natural unit system used in the T0 model, which is based on Planck units with energy as the fundamental dimension. The key insight is that energy \([E]\) serves as the universal dimension from which all other physical quantities derive.

2.1 Comparison with Other Natural Unit Systems

3 Fundamentals of Natural Unit Systems

3.1 Planck Units

The Planck units were proposed by Max Planck in 1899 and are based on the fundamental natural constants:

\[\begin{aligned} G &= 1 \quad \text{(gravitational constant)} \\ c &= 1 \quad \text{(speed of light)} \\ \hbar &= 1 \quad \text{(reduced Planck constant)} \end{aligned}\](1)

Planck recognized that these units “retain their meaning for all times and for all, including extraterrestrial and non-human cultures necessarily” .

3.2 Atomic Units

The atomic units, introduced by Hartree in 1927 , set:

\[\begin{aligned} m_e &= 1 \quad \text{(electron mass)} \\ e &= 1 \quad \text{(elementary charge)} \\ \hbar &= 1 \\ (1)/(4πε_0) &= 1 \quad \text{(Coulomb constant)} \end{aligned}\](2)

3.3 Quantum Optical Units

For quantum field theory applications, quantum optical units are commonly used:

\[\begin{aligned} c &= 1 \quad \text{(speed of light)} \\ \hbar &= 1 \quad \text{(reduced Planck constant)} \\ ε_0 &= 1 \quad \text{(permittivity)} \\ μ_0 &= 1 \quad \text{(permeability, because } c = 1/\sqrt{ε_0 μ_0}\text{)} \end{aligned}\](3)

3.4 Advantages of Natural Units

Natural units offer several key advantages:

  • **Simplified equations** (e.g., \(E = m\) instead of \(E = mc^2\))

  • **No superfluous constants** in calculations

  • **Universal scaling** for fundamental physics

  • **Reveals fundamental relationships** between physical quantities

  • **Provides dimensional consistency** checks

  • **Eliminates arbitrary conversion factors**

  • **Highlights the universal role** of energy

4 Mathematical Proof of Energy Equivalence

4.1 Fundamental Dimensional Relations

In natural units, all physical quantities have dimensions that can be expressed as powers of energy \([E]\) :

\[\begin{aligned} [L] &= [E]^{-1} \quad \text{(from } \hbar c = 1\text{)} \\ [T] &= [E]^{-1} \quad \text{(from } \hbar = 1\text{)} \\ [M] &= [E] \quad \text{(from } c = 1\text{)} \end{aligned}\](4)

4.2 Conversion of Fundamental Quantities

Length: From the relation \(\hbar c = 1\) it follows:

\[[L] = ([\hbar][c])/([E]) = [E]^{-1}\](5)

Time: From \(\hbar = 1\) and \(E = \hbar ω\) it follows:

\[[T] = ([\hbar])/([E]) = [E]^{-1}\](6)

Mass: From \(E = mc^2\) and \(c = 1\) it follows:

\[[M] = [E]\](7)

Velocity:

\[[v] = ([L])/([T]) = \frac{[E]^{-1}}{[E]^{-1}} = [E]^0 = \text{dimensionless}\](8)

Momentum:

\[[p] = [M][v] = [E] · [E]^0 = [E]\](9)

Force:

\[[F] = [M][a] = [E] · [E]^{-1} = [E]^2\](10)

Charge: In Planck units from \(F = (1)/(4πε_0) (q^2)/(r^2)\):

\[[q] = [E]^{1/2}\](11)

4.3 Generalization

Any physical quantity \(G\) can be represented as a product of powers of the fundamental constants:

\[G = c^a · \hbar^b · G^c · k_B^d · \ldots\](12)

In natural units this becomes:

\[[G] = [E]^n \quad \text{for a specific } n ∈ \mathbb{Q}\](13)

4.4 Fundamental Relationships

The key relationships in natural units become:

\[\begin{aligned} E &= m \quad \text{(mass-energy equivalence)} \\ E &= T \quad \text{(temperature-energy equivalence)} \\ [L] &= [T] = [E^{-1}] \quad \text{(space-time unity)} \\ ω &= E \quad \text{(frequency-energy equivalence)} \\ p &= E \quad \text{(momentum-energy equivalence for massless particles)} \end{aligned}\](14)
