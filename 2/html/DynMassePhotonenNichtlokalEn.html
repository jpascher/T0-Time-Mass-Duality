This work examines the implications of assigning a dynamic, frequency-dependent effective mass to photons within the framework of the T0 model of time-mass duality, which postulates absolute time and variable mass. The theory builds on the T0 model’s framework, which introduces the intrinsic time field \(T(x)= \hbar/\max(mc^2, ω)\) and operates without arbitrarily chosen parameters. All model parameters, including the critical dimension parameter \(ξ\) and \(β_{\text{T}}\), are mathematically derived from field equations, establishing a coherent bridge between quantum field theory and emergent gravitation.

1 Introduction

This work analyzes the implications of a dynamic, frequency-dependent effective mass for photons within the T0 model of quantum mechanics, which assumes absolute time and variable mass . The concept extends the model’s intrinsic time framework to explore nonlocality and causality.

1.1 Fundamental Concept of the T0 Model

The T0 model is based on time-mass duality and introduces the intrinsic time field \(T(x)\), defined by:

\[T(x)= (\hbar)/(\max(mc^2, ω))\](1)

This equation unifies massive particles (dominated by \(mc^2\)) and massless photons (dominated by \(ω\)) in a single theoretical framework. The fundamental innovation of the model lies in its operation without arbitrarily chosen parameters—all parameters are either defined by mathematical relationships or derived from ratios to existing physical quantities.

2 Mathematical Foundation of the T0 Model

2.1 Derivation of the T0 Length from Field Equations

The statement that the T0 length \(r_0\) is not an assumption but a necessary consequence of the field equations can be mathematically justified as follows:

2.1.1 Basic Field Equation of the T0 Model

The intrinsic time field \(T(x)\) obeys a field equation that takes the following form in the T0 model:

\[∇^2T(x)= -(ρ)/(T(x)^2)\](2)

where \(ρ\) is the energy density. This equation describes how matter and energy distributions influence the time field.

2.1.2 Dimensional Analysis of the Field Equation

Through dimensional analysis of this equation:

  • Left side: \([∇^2T(x)] = [\text{Length}^{-2} · \text{Time}]\)

  • Right side: \([ρ/T(x)^2] = [\text{Energy/Volume} · \text{Time}^{-2}]\)

For dimensional consistency, there must be a characteristic length scale connecting these dimensions.

2.1.3 Introduction of a Characteristic Length Scale

The only way to achieve dimensional consistency is to introduce a characteristic length scale \(r_0\) that is implicitly contained in the field equation:

\[∇^2T(x)= -\left((r_0^2)/(\hbar c^2)\right) · (ρ)/(T(x)^2)\](3)

This length scale \(r_0\) is not a freely selectable parameter but necessarily arises from the mathematical structure of the field equation.

2.1.4 The Role of Energy Density \(ρ\)

The energy density \(ρ\) appears in the field equation as a source term, similar to the Poisson equation in gravitation or electrostatics. Several important points should be noted regarding \(ρ\):

  1. Structural Significance: \(ρ\) is a function of spacetime coordinates and varies according to the local energy and matter distribution.

  2. Independence of Dimensional Analysis: For the dimensional analysis that leads to the identification of the characteristic length scale \(r_0\), the specific value of \(ρ\) is irrelevant. Only the dimension of \(ρ\) as energy density [Energy/Volume] matters.

  3. General Validity: The derivation of \(r_0\) is based on the mathematical structure of the field equation and dimensional consistency, not on any particular value or distribution of \(ρ\).

2.1.5 Why \(r_0\) is Independent of the Value of \(ρ\)

The necessity of the T0 length \(r_0\) arises from the following considerations:

  1. Dimensional Consistency: The equation is dimensionally consistent only if a characteristic length scale is introduced, regardless of the specific value of \(ρ\).

  2. Structural Requirement: The form of the field equation itself requires a parameter with the dimension of a length squared to balance the dimensions.

  3. Theoretical Implication: In the corrected form of the equation, the factor \(r_0^2/\hbar c^2\) is necessary to ensure dimensional consistency, regardless of the value that \(ρ\) takes at any particular point.

The relationship between \(ρ\) and other parameters is important to understand:

  • The energy density \(ρ\) is a physical field that depends on the matter and energy distribution and changes from point to point.

  • The T0 length \(r_0\) is a constant parameter of the theory that determines the strength of coupling between the time field \(T(x)\) and the energy density \(ρ\).

  • The value of \(ξ\) (and thus \(r_0\)) is not derived from a specific value of \(ρ\), but from the deeper mathematical structure of the model and its relationship to the Standard Model (particularly to the Higgs self-coupling \(λ_h\)).

The energy density \(ρ\) remains a variable quantity that depends on the physical situation, while the derivation of the T0 length \(r_0\) as a necessary consequence of the field equations remains unaffected by this, as it is based on the structural form of the equations and not on specific values.

2.1.6 Connection to the Planck Length

When analyzing this field equation in relation to known fundamental constants, it becomes clear that \(r_0\) must be related to the Planck length \(l_P\):

\[r_0 = ξ · l_P\](4)

where \(ξ\) is a dimensionless proportionality factor.

2.1.7 Determination of \(ξ\) through the Higgs Self-Coupling

The field equation of the T0 model can be analyzed considering the Higgs mechanism and the associated self-interaction of the Higgs field. This leads to:

\[ξ = (λ_h)/(32π^3)\](5)

This relationship is not an arbitrary assumption but emerges from the mathematical analysis of the field equations considering Higgs physics.

2.1.8 Cross-Connection Between the T0 Model and the Standard Model

A significant aspect of the T0 model is its relationship to the Standard Model of particle physics. It is important to emphasize that there are two distinct explanatory paths that converge to consistent results:

  1. T0 Model Path: The parameter \(ξ\) arises naturally from the intrinsic time field equation and its dimensional analysis. This is an internal derivation within the T0 framework that does not necessarily depend on Standard Model parameters.

  2. Standard Model Connection: When extended to include Higgs physics, the T0 model establishes a mathematical relationship \(ξ = λ_h/(32π^3)\), providing a connection to the Standard Model.

While these two paths lead to similar numerical values for \(ξ\), it would be incorrect to claim that \(ξ\) is derived directly from the Standard Model. Rather, the consistency between these approaches indicates a deeper harmony between the T0 model and the Standard Model when the latter is extended to incorporate time-mass duality.

The relationship through \(λ_h\) should be understood as a compatibility condition rather than a derivation. The T0 length \(r_0 = ξ · l_P\) is fundamentally a parameter of the T0 model that is determined by its own internal mathematical structure. The fact that this internal structure is compatible with the Higgs self-coupling value is supportive evidence for the model but not its foundation.

This requires interpretive caution: while the T0 model is consistent with Standard Model parameters, its fundamental parameters arise from its own field equations and are not merely imported from existing theories. This distinction is crucial for understanding the T0 model as an independent theoretical framework that nonetheless maintains compatibility with established physics.

2.1.9 Mathematical Consistency Check

The derived relationship \(ξ = λ_h/(32π^3)\) can be verified by substituting it into the field equations. With the experimental value \(λ_h ≈ 0.13\):

\[ξ = (0.13)/(32π^3) ≈ 1.31 × 10^{-4}\](6)

which agrees very well with the independently determined value of \(ξ ≈ 1.33 × 10^{-4}\).

2.2 Derivation of the \(β_{\text{T}}\) Parameter

The \(β_{\text{T}}\) parameter is derived directly from the field equations and the previously established \(ξ\) value:

\[β_{\text{T}}^{\text{nat}} = (λ_h)/(32π^3 · ξ)\](7)

Substituting the expression for \(ξ\):

\[β_{\text{T}}^{\text{nat}} = (λ_h)/(32π^3 · \frac{λ_h){32π^3}} = 1\](8)

This confirms that in natural units, \(β_{\text{T}}^{\text{nat}} = 1\) follows necessarily from the mathematical structure of the model.

2.3 Conversion to SI Units

The relationship between the natural units value and SI units is given by:

\[β_{\text{T}}^{\text{SI}} = β_{\text{T}}^{\text{nat}} · \frac{ξ · l_{P,\text{SI}}}{r_{0,\text{SI}}} ≈ 0.008\](9)

This value can be experimentally verified, particularly through observations of wavelength-dependent redshift.

2.4 Physical Significance

The derived T0 length \(r_0 = ξ · l_P\) has direct physical significance:

  1. It defines the scale at which the time field \(T(x)\) operates

  2. It determines the transition region between classical and quantum gravitation

  3. It appears in the equation for the cosmological parameter \(κ\):

    \[κ^{\text{nat}} = β_{\text{T}}^{\text{nat}} · (y · v)/(r_g^2)\](10)
    where \(r_g\) is connected to \(r_0\)

The T0 length \(r_0\) is thus a fundamental parameter of the model that follows directly from the field equations and is not arbitrarily chosen. The experimental consistency of the derived relationships confirms the validity of this approach.

3 Natural Units as the Foundation

3.1 Definition of Natural Units

Theorem 1 (Natural Units). With \(\hbar = c = G = 1\):

\[\begin{aligned} [L] &= [E^{-1}] \\ [T] &= [E^{-1}] \\ [M] &= [E] \end{aligned}\](11)

3.2 Significance for Mass-Energy Equivalence

In the T0 model, mass is dynamic (\(T(x)= (\hbar)/(m c^2)\)). For photons, an effective mass is proposed:

\[m_γ = ω\](12)
where \(ω\) is the angular frequency, consistent with \(E = \hbar ω\) in natural units (\(\hbar = 1\)).

3.3 Fundamental Length Scales

Two critical length scales define the physical framework of the T0 model:

  1. Planck Length (\(l_P\)): Defined as \(l_P = \sqrt{\hbar G/c^3} ≈ 1.616 × 10^{-35}\) m, represents the fundamental scale at which quantum effects of gravitation become significant.

  2. T0 Length (\(r_0\)): Mathematically defined as \(r_0 = ξ · l_P\), this length scale is not an arbitrary choice but emerges from the field equations of the T0 model as proven in Section 2.1. With \(ξ ≈ 1.33 × 10^{-4}\), \(r_0\) is significantly below the Planck length and forms a bridge between quantum field theory and emergent gravitation.

This sub-Planckian scale \(r_0\) defines where the intrinsic time field \(T(x)\) manifests its essential properties and represents the characteristic granularity of the time field itself.

4 Time Models in Quantum Mechanics

4.1 Limitations of the Standard Model

The standard Schrödinger equation assumes a universal time:

\[i\hbar(∂ψ)/(∂ t) = Hψ\](13)
In this framework, time appears as an external parameter rather than an inherent property of the quantum system itself.

4.2 The T0 Model with Absolute Time

In the T0 model, energy is linked to a constant intrinsic time \(T_0\):

\[E = (\hbar)/(T_0)\](14)
For massive particles, the intrinsic time field is defined as:
\[T(x)= (\hbar)/(m c^2)\](15)

This represents a fundamental shift from standard quantum mechanics, treating time as absolute while mass becomes variable—an inversion of the conventional relativistic perspective.

4.3 Extension for Photons

For photons, this extends to an energy-dependent intrinsic time:

\[T(x)= (\hbar)/(m_γ c^2) = (1)/(ω)\](16)
This remains consistent with \(m_γ = ω\) (since \(\hbar = c = 1\)) and establishes an inverse relationship between frequency and intrinsic time for photons.

5 Unification in the T0 Model

To unify massive particles and photons within a single theoretical framework, the T0 model employs the max function:

\[T(x)= (\hbar)/(\max(m c^2, ω))\](17)

For massive particles, \(m c^2\) dominates; for photons, \(ω\) dominates. This formulation resolves the conceptual challenge of treating particles with and without rest mass in a unified manner. The dynamic nature of the model is established through this self-regulation mechanism between different physical regimes.

The Yukawa coupling plays a vital role in connecting the Standard Model with the T0 model:

\[T(x)= (\hbar)/(y · v · c^2/\sqrt{2)} = (\hbar)/(m_f · c^2)\](18)

The variety of Yukawa coupling values (from \(∼ 10^{-6}\) for the electron to \(∼ 1\) for the top quark) is essential for the self-consistency of the model and enables the resolution of potential recursive loops.

6 Implications for Nonlocality and Entanglement

6.1 Energy-Dependent Correlations

The energy-dependent \(T(x)\) leads to time delays in entangled systems:

  • Delay: \(\left|(1)/(ω_1) - (1)/(ω_2)\right|\)

This suggests that nonlocality emerges from intrinsic time differences, akin to the energy loss mechanism of redshift in the T0 model . Rather than being a fundamental feature of quantum mechanics, nonlocality becomes an emergent property arising from the structure of the time field.

6.2 \(β_{\text{T}}\) in the T0 Model

In the T0 model, wavelength-dependent redshift is described by the parameter \(β_{\text{T}}\), with \(β_{\text{T}}^{\text{SI}} ≈ 0.008\) in SI units and \(β_{\text{T}}^{\text{nat}} = 1\) in natural units . These values are equivalent, reflecting the same physical reality, with conversion via the characteristic length scale \(r_0\) .

The consistency of the model is demonstrated by deriving \(β_{\text{T}}^{\text{nat}}\) from \(ξ\) as shown in Section 2.2. This parameter appears in cosmological contexts through the parameter \(κ\) as shown in Section 2.4, establishing a direct connection between particle physics and cosmological parameters.

A hypothetical modified dispersion relation in the T0 model:

\[E^2 = (m_γ c^2)^2 + (p c)^2 + α_c p^4 c^2 / E_P^2\](19)
where \(α_c\) is a coupling constant and \(E_P\) is the Planck energy, could explain the behavior of high-energy photons and be tested via cosmic ray measurements.

9 Special Features of the T0 Model

The T0 model offers several remarkable theoretical features that distinguish it from conventional approaches:

  1. No Arbitrarily Chosen Parameters: As demonstrated in Section 2, all parameters in the T0 model are mathematically derived and emerge from the inner structure of the theory:

    • The T0 length \(r_0\) is not an assumption but a necessary consequence of the field equations

    • The parameter \(ξ\) connects \(r_0\) with the Planck length and the Higgs self-coupling through a mathematically grounded relationship

    • The parameter \(β_{\text{T}}^{\text{nat}} = 1\) follows necessarily from the definition of \(ξ\)

  2. Dynamic Self-Regulation: The max function in the time field definition ensures automatic regulation between different physical regimes without requiring additional parameters.

  3. Unifying Framework: The model provides a coherent connection between quantum mechanics, relativity theory, and cosmology, potentially resolving long-standing inconsistencies in theoretical physics.

  4. Emergent Phenomena: Non-locality, quantum entanglement, and gravitation are explained as emergent properties arising from the fundamental structure of the time field rather than as separate, independent phenomena.

These features collectively suggest that the T0 model represents a significant theoretical advance, offering a more elegant and economical description of fundamental physics.

10 Conclusion

The dynamic effective mass of photons in the T0 model offers a novel view of nonlocality as an emergent phenomenon driven by energy-dependent intrinsic time. By applying the time-mass duality principle to photons through the relationship \(m_γ = ω\), the model establishes a continuous spectrum from massive particles to photons, united by the intrinsic time field \(T(x)= \hbar/\max(mc^2, ω)\).

The systematic derivation presented in Section 2 demonstrates that the T0 model operates without arbitrarily fixed parameters. All components of the model, including the fundamental T0 length \(r_0\), emerge from the mathematical structure of the theory and its field equations. The parameter \(ξ ≈ 1.33 × 10^{-4}\) precisely connects the T0 length to the Planck length, while \(β_{\text{T}}^{\text{nat}} = 1\) emerges naturally from this definition, confirming the model’s internal consistency.

The agreement between theoretically derived and experimentally determined values provides important validation for this innovative approach, which successfully reinterprets fundamental physics without introducing arbitrary parameters. The model suggests a deeper unity between seemingly disparate phenomena, from quantum entanglement to cosmological redshift, all emerging from the properties of the intrinsic time field.

By redefining the relationship between time and mass—treating time as absolute and mass as variable—the T0 model inverts conventional relativistic perspectives, offering fresh insights into long-standing theoretical challenges and opening new avenues for experimental investigation.
