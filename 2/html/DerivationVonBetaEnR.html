1 Natural Units Framework and Dimensional Analysis

Natural unit systems have been fundamental to theoretical physics since Planck’s seminal work in 1899 . The basic principle involves setting fundamental physical constants to unity to reveal the underlying mathematical structure of physical laws .

1.1 The Unit System

Following the convention established in quantum field theory and quantum optics , we set:

  • \(\hbar = 1\) (reduced Planck constant)

  • \(c = 1\) (speed of light)

  • \(α_{EM} = 1\) (fine-structure constant, as discussed in )

This choice reduces all physical quantities to energy dimensions, following the approach pioneered by Dirac and extensively used in modern particle physics .

1.2 Historical Development and Theoretical Foundation

The use of natural units in fundamental physics has deep historical roots:

Planck Era (1899-1906): Max Planck introduced the first natural unit system based on \(\hbar\), \(c\), and \(G\) , recognizing that these units would "retain their meaning for all times and for all, including extraterrestrial and non-human cultures" .

Atomic Units (1927): Hartree developed atomic units for quantum chemistry applications , setting \(m_e = e = \hbar = 1/(4πε_0) = 1\).

Particle Physics Era (1950s-present): The modern approach in high-energy physics typically uses \(\hbar = c = 1\) , with energy measured in GeV.

Quantum Field Theory: Comprehensive treatments by establish the standard framework we follow here.

\[\label{eq:field_equation_fundamental} ∇^2 m(x,t) = 4π G ρ(x,t) · m(x,t)\](1)

3 Geometric Derivation of the \(β\) Parameter

The geometric approach follows the methodology established in general relativity for solving Einstein’s field equations .

3.1 Spherically Symmetric Solutions

For a point mass source, we employ the same techniques used for the Schwarzschild solution :

\[ρ(x) = m · δ^3(\vec{x})\](3)

The spherically symmetric Laplacian operator, as detailed in and , gives:

\[∇^2 m(r) = (1)/(r^2)(d)/(dr)\left(r^2 (dm)/(dr)\right)\](4)

Outside the source (\(r > 0\)), following the standard Green’s function approach :

\[(1)/(r^2)(d)/(dr)\left(r^2 (dm)/(dr)\right) = 0\](5)

The solution methodology parallels that used for electrostatic potentials and gravitational potentials .

3.2 Boundary Conditions and Physical Interpretation

Following the approach of for boundary value problems in general relativity:

Asymptotic condition: \(\lim_{r \to ∞} T(r) = T_0\), ensuring finite values at infinity, analogous to the asymptotic flatness condition in GR .

Near-origin behavior: Using Gauss’s theorem :

\[\oint_S ∇ m · d\vec{S} = 4π G ∈t_V ρ(x) m(x) \, dV\](6)

The factor of 2 emergence follows from relativistic corrections, similar to how the Schwarzschild radius \(r_s = 2GM/c^2\) emerges in general relativity .

3.3 The Characteristic Length Scale

\[\boxed{r_0 = 2Gm}\](7)
