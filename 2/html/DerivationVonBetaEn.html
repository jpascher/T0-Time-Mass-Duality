\[\label{eq:time_field_fundamental} T(x,t) = (1)/(\max(m(x,t), ω))\](1)

Physical interpretation: The time field is inversely proportional to the characteristic energy scale (mass for massive particles, frequency for photons). This reflects the fundamental time-mass duality of the T0 model, where time and mass are inversely related.

3 Geometric Derivation of the \(β\) Parameter

3.1 Point Particle Source

To derive \(β\), we first consider the simplest case: a point particle with mass \(m\) at the origin:

\[\label{eq:point_source_beta} ρ(x) = m · δ^3(\vec{x})\](4)

Dimensional verification:

  • \([ρ(x)] = [E^4]\) (energy density)

  • \([m] = [E]\) (mass energy)

  • \([δ^3(\vec{x})] = [1/L^3] = [E^3]\) (delta function)

  • \([m · δ^3(\vec{x})] = [E · E^3] = [E^4]\)

3.2 Spherically Symmetric Solution

The solution outside the origin (\(r > 0\)) is:

\[T(r) = (1)/(m)\left(1 - (r_0)/(r)\right)\](5)

where \(r_0 = 2Gm\) is the characteristic length of the T0 model, exactly corresponding to the Schwarzschild radius.

Dimensional consistency check:

  • \([T(r)] = [1/m] · [1 - 2Gm/r]\)

    • \([1/m] = [E^{-1}]\)

    • \([2Gm/r] = [E^{-2} · E · E] = [1]\) (dimensionless)

  • Therefore \([T(r)] = [E^{-1}]\)

3.3 Definition of \(β\)

At this point, we define the dimensionless parameter \(β\) as:

\[\label{eq:beta_definition} β = (r_0)/(r) = (2Gm)/(r)\](6)

Dimensional analysis:

  • \([r_0] = [2Gm] = [E^{-2} · E] = [E^{-1}]\) (characteristic length)

  • \([r] = [E^{-1}]\) (distance)

  • \([β] = [r_0/r] = [E^{-1}/E^{-1}] = [1]\) (dimensionless)

With this definition, we can express the time field more elegantly as:

\[\label{eq:time_field_with_beta} T(r) = (1)/(m)(1 - β)\](7)

10.1 Geometric Derivation of the T0 Characteristic Length \(r_0\)

10.1.1 Step-by-Step Geometric Derivation

Building upon our field-theoretic foundation, we now provide the complete geometric derivation of the characteristic length \(r_0\).

Starting from the fundamental field equation:

\[∇^2 m(r) = 4π G ρ(r) · m(r)\](20)

For a point mass \(m\) at origin: \(ρ(r) = m · δ^3(\vec{r})\)

Outside the origin (\(r > 0\)), where \(ρ = 0\):

\[(1)/(r^2)(d)/(dr)\left(r^2 (dm)/(dr)\right) = 0\](21)

First integration:

\[r^2 (dm)/(dr) = C_1 \quad \Rightarrow \quad (dm)/(dr) = (C_1)/(r^2)\](22)

Second integration:

\[m(r) = A - (C_1)/(r)\](23)

Boundary condition 1: \(\lim_{r \to ∞} m(r) = m_0\) (asymptotic mass) Therefore: \(A = m_0\)

Boundary condition 2: Using Gauss’s theorem around the point source:

\[\oint_S ∇ m · d\vec{S} = 4π G ∈t_V ρ(r) m(r) \, dV\](24)

For small radius \(ε\):

\[4π ε^2 \left.(dm)/(dr)\right|_{r=ε} = 4π G m · m_0\](25)

With \(dm/dr = C_1/r^2\):

\[4π ε^2 · (C_1)/(ε^2) = 4π G m · m_0\](26)

Therefore: \(C_1 = G m · m_0\)

Complete solution:

\[m(r) = m_0\left(1 + (Gm)/(r)\right)\](27)

10.1.2 Physical Origin of the Factor 2

The factor 2 in \(r_0 = 2Gm\) arises from the geometric structure of the T0 field equation:

  1. The field equation \(∇^2 m = 4π G ρ m\) has a specific Green’s function structure

  3. The boundary conditions at origin and infinity determine the coefficient

  4. Full relativistic field theory (considering second-order effects) doubles the Newtonian result

Mathematical verification: The relativistic correction emerges from higher-order terms in the field expansion. The full T0 field equation in the relativistic regime becomes:

\[∇^2 m = 4π G ρ m \left(1 + (T_0 - T)/(T_0)\right)\](28)

This self-consistency condition requires the factor 2 for mathematical consistency.

Geometric characteristic length: From this solution, we identify the natural characteristic length scale:

\[\boxed{r_0 = 2Gm}\](29)
