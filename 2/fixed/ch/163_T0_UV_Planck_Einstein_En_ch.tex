\section{The Ultraviolet Catastrophe: A Precise Diagnosis}
\label{sec:uv_catastrophe}
% ==============================================================================

\subsection{The Blackbody Radiation Problem}

Imagine a completely closed cavity whose walls have been heated uniformly to 
temperature $T$ and are in thermal equilibrium. If a tiny hole is drilled in the 
wall, radiation escapes. This \emph{cavity radiation} (also: \emph{blackbody 
radiation}) possesses a remarkable property: its spectrum depends \emph{solely} on 
the temperature — not on the material or shape of the cavity.

The experimentally measured quantity is the \emph{spectral energy density} $\rho(f, T)$: 
the energy per unit volume per unit frequency interval. At different temperatures, 
one obtains characteristic curves with clear signatures:
\begin{itemize}
    \item The peak of the curve shifts to higher frequencies as temperature increases 
    (Wien's displacement law).
    \item The total area under the curve grows as the fourth power of temperature 
    (Stefan-Boltzmann law: $u \propto T^4$).
    \item A hot piece of metal glows first dull red, then brighter orange-yellow, 
    finally white-hot — the dominant frequency visibly shifts upward.
\end{itemize}

The challenge for theoretical physics around 1900 was precise: derive this curve 
from the fundamental laws of physics.

\subsection{The Classical Approach and Its Failure}

The natural starting point was Maxwell's theory combined with the well-established 
statistical mechanics. The approach is conceptually simple:

\paragraph{Step 1: Counting modes.}
Inside the cavity, only standing electromagnetic waves can form — precisely those 
wavelengths for which an integer number of half-wavelengths fit between the walls. 
The number of electromagnetic modes per unit volume in the frequency interval 
$[f, f + df]$ is:

\begin{equation}
    g(f) \, df = \frac{8\pi f^2}{c^3} \, df
    \label{eq:mode_density}
\end{equation}

The crucial point: mode density grows quadratically with frequency. For every 
doubling of frequency, there are four times as many independent wave patterns. 
And there is \emph{no upper limit} — wavelength can become arbitrarily small, 
frequency arbitrarily large.

\paragraph{Step 2: Equipartition theorem.}
Classical statistical mechanics (Boltzmann, Maxwell) yields the equipartition 
theorem: in thermal equilibrium at temperature $T$, each quadratic degree of freedom 
carries on average the energy $\frac{1}{2} k_B T$. An electromagnetic wave has two 
quadratic degrees of freedom (electric and magnetic field), so each mode carries on 
average:

\begin{equation}
    \langle E_{\text{mode}} \rangle = k_B T
    \label{eq:equipartition}
\end{equation}

This holds \emph{independently of the frequency of the mode}. A mode at $10^{15}$ Hz 
receives the same average energy as a mode at $10^3$ Hz.

\paragraph{Step 3: Spectral energy density.}
The spectral energy density follows as a simple product:

\begin{equation}
    \rho_{\text{class}}(f, T) = g(f) \cdot \langle E_{\text{mode}} \rangle 
    = \frac{8\pi f^2}{c^3} \cdot k_B T
    \label{eq:rayleigh_jeans}
\end{equation}

This is the \emph{Rayleigh-Jeans law}. It agrees well with experiment at low 
frequencies. At high frequencies, however, it diverges catastrophically.

\subsection{The Scale of the Catastrophe}

The total energy density is obtained by integrating over all frequencies:

\begin{equation}
    u_{\text{class}} = \int_0^\infty \rho_{\text{class}}(f, T) \, df 
    = \frac{8\pi k_B T}{c^3} \int_0^\infty f^2 \, df = \infty
    \label{eq:uv_divergence}
\end{equation}

The integral diverges. Classical physics predicts: a cavity in thermal equilibrium 
contains \emph{infinite energy}. This is physically absurd.

This divergence is called the \textbf{ultraviolet catastrophe} because it manifests 
most dramatically at the high-frequency (ultraviolet and beyond) end of the spectrum. 
The name is somewhat misleading — the divergence actually extends across the entire 
high-frequency spectrum, not just the ultraviolet range.

\begin{important}[Why the Failure Is Deep]
    The Rayleigh-Jeans law is not a calculational error. It is the correct application 
    of two well-confirmed theories: Maxwell's electrodynamics and Boltzmann's 
    statistical mechanics. Its failure therefore shows that one of these foundations 
    must be fundamentally wrong — or that an important physical fact is missing.
\end{important}

\subsection{The Two Empirical Partial Solutions}

Before the complete solution, there were two important empirical clues:

\paragraph{The Rayleigh-Jeans law} (derived around 1900) fits well at \emph{low} 
frequencies:
\begin{equation}
    \rho_{\text{RJ}}(f, T) \approx \frac{8\pi f^2}{c^3} k_B T \quad \text{(for } f \to 0\text{)}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Wien's radiation law} (1896) describes \emph{high} frequencies well:
\begin{equation}
    \rho_{\text{Wien}}(f, T) = af^3 \exp\!\left(-\frac{bf}{T}\right)
    \label{eq:wien_law}
\end{equation}

where $a$ and $b$ are empirical constants. At high frequencies, the exponential 
falls off faster than $f^2$ grows — the integral converges.

The problem: both laws hold only in their respective limiting regimes. Between them 
lie the experimentally determined data, showing a smooth connection. A unified theory 
was missing.

\section{Planck's Solution: An Act of Desperation}
\label{sec:planck}
% ==============================================================================

\subsection{Planck's Strategy}

Max Planck approached the problem differently from his contemporaries. Rather than 
starting from a specific microscopic model, he worked \emph{thermodynamically 
backwards}: he searched for the mathematical expression for the entropy of cavity 
radiation that was consistent with the empirically known spectrum.

The decisive step: Planck found a formula that reduces to the Rayleigh-Jeans law at 
low frequencies and to Wien's law at high frequencies:

\begin{equation}
    \rho_{\text{Planck}}(f, T) = \frac{8\pi h f^3}{c^3} \cdot \frac{1}{\exp\!\left(\dfrac{hf}{k_BT}\right) - 1}
    \label{eq:planck_law}
\end{equation}

This formula describes the experimental spectrum across the entire frequency range 
with extraordinary precision — with only one new constant: Planck's constant $h$.

\subsection{The Price: Quantization as a Postulate}

To derive his formula, Planck had to make an assumption that he himself described as 
an \emph{act of desperation}: he assumed that energy can only be exchanged in 
\emph{discrete multiples} of $hf$. The energy of a single portion (\emph{quantum}) is:

\begin{equation}
    \epsilon = hf
    \label{eq:planck_quantum}
\end{equation}

With this assumption, the average energy of a mode at frequency $f$ is no longer 
$k_BT$ (equipartition theorem) but:

\begin{equation}
    \langle E_{\text{mode}} \rangle = \frac{hf}{\exp\!\left(\dfrac{hf}{k_BT}\right) - 1}
    \label{eq:planck_mean_energy}
\end{equation}

For $hf \ll k_BT$ (low frequencies), this expression approaches $k_BT$ — the 
classical result is recovered. For $hf \gg k_BT$ (high frequencies), the average 
energy falls off exponentially — exactly what is needed to suppress the divergence.

\subsection{What Planck Did Not Explain}

Quantization solved the spectral problem mathematically. But Planck himself regarded 
it as a computational auxiliary construct, not a physical statement about the nature 
of light. In his view, the electromagnetic field remained a continuous wave. 
Quantization was a property of the \emph{interaction} between matter and radiation — 
not of radiation itself.

\begin{keyresult}[Planck's Open Question]
    Planck had found the curve. But he had not explained \emph{why} energy is 
    exchanged in discrete portions. The number $h$ was a free parameter — measured 
    from the data, not derived from deeper principles. What is $h$? Why exactly 
    this value?
\end{keyresult}

\subsection{The Structural Problem}

On one side stands the theory of matter: gases and other ponderable bodies are

On the other side stands Maxwell's theory: the state of the electromagnetic field

In Einstein's own words: there is a \emph{profound formal distinction} between

\subsection{The Thermodynamic Derivation}

\paragraph{Step 1: Entropy of radiation.}
Einstein introduces a \emph{spectral entropy density} $\sigma(f, T)$ — analogous 
to the spectral energy density $\rho(f, T)$. In thermal equilibrium, the 
thermodynamic relation holds:

\begin{equation}
    \frac{\partial \sigma}{\partial \rho} = \frac{1}{T}
    \label{eq:thermo_relation}
\end{equation}

This is not a model assumption but a fundamental thermodynamic identity.

\paragraph{Step 2: Entropy from Wien's law.}
By substituting Wien's law \eqref{eq:wien_law} into the thermodynamic relation 
\eqref{eq:thermo_relation} and integrating, Einstein obtains the entropy density 
in the Wien regime:

\begin{equation}
    \sigma(f, T) = -\frac{\rho}{bf} \left[\ln\!\left(\frac{\rho}{af^3}\right) - 1\right]
    \label{eq:entropy_density}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Step 3: Entropy change under volume change.}
Einstein now considers a narrow, quasi-monochromatic slice of radiation with total 
energy $E$ at frequency $f$, enclosed in volume $V_0$. He calculates the entropy 
change when this volume is changed to $V$:

\begin{equation}
    \Delta S = S(V) - S(V_0) = \frac{E}{bf} \ln\!\left(\frac{V}{V_0}\right)
    \label{eq:entropy_change}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Step 4: Comparison with an ideal gas.}
This is the decisive moment. For an ideal gas of $N$ independently moving particles, 
Boltzmann's entropy formula $S = k_B \ln W$ (with $W$ as the number of microstates) 
yields:

\begin{equation}
    \Delta S_{\text{gas}} = N k_B \ln\!\left(\frac{V}{V_0}\right)
    \label{eq:entropy_gas}
\end{equation}

The two expressions \eqref{eq:entropy_change} and \eqref{eq:entropy_gas} have 
\emph{identical structure}. Comparing the exponents gives:

\begin{equation}
    \frac{E}{bf} = N k_B \quad \Longrightarrow \quad N = \frac{E}{k_B b f}
    \label{eq:quanta_count}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Step 5: Energy of one quantum.}
If the total energy $E$ is carried by $N$ independent entities, each carrying energy 
$\epsilon$, then $E = N\epsilon$, so:

\begin{equation}
    \epsilon = \frac{E}{N} = k_B b f
    \label{eq:quantum_energy_wien}
\end{equation}

Since Wien's constant $b$ turns out to be $b = h/k_B$ by comparison with Planck's 
law, we finally obtain:

\begin{equation}
    \boxed{\epsilon = hf}
    \label{eq:quantum_energy_final}
\end{equation}

\begin{revolutionary}[Einstein's Decisive Insight]
    The light quantum was not postulated. It was \emph{forced} by thermodynamics. 
    The only inputs were: (1) the empirical Wien law, (2) the thermodynamic

\end{revolutionary}

\subsection{The Photoelectric Effect as Confirmation}

Only after this derivation does Einstein apply his hypothesis to the photoelectric 
effect. The result is the famous equation:

\begin{equation}
    E_{\text{kin, max}} = hf - \Phi
    \label{eq:photoelectric}
\end{equation}

where $\Phi$ is the \emph{work function} of the metal. This equation is linear in $f$ 
with slope $h$ — a very sharp, experimentally testable prediction.

Experimental confirmation came through Robert Millikan (1916), who despite years of
