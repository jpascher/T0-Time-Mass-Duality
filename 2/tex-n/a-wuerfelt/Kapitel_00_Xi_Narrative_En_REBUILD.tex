When Einstein wrote to Max Born in 1926 that he was convinced \emph{the Old One} 
does not play dice, he expressed a deep intuition: that the laws of nature 
cannot be built on chance but on a hidden order. Quantum mechanics seemed to 
prove him right -- and to contradict him at the same time. The wave function 
described the world with uncanny precision, but the constants entering its 
equations remained unexplained numbers. Why $1/137$? Why does the electron 
weigh exactly $0.511\,$MeV? Why is gravitation weaker than the electromagnetic 
force by a factor of $10^{36}$?

For nearly a century, physics treated these questions as unanswerable -- or at 
least not answerable within existing theories. The Standard Model of particle 
physics, the most successful theory of all time, describes nature with 
breathtaking precision. But it contains 19 free parameters that do not follow 
from principles but must be measured and inserted. It \emph{describes}, but it 
does not \emph{explain}.
