	\chapter*{Introduction to Part 2: Mathematical Foundations and Derivations}
	\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Introduction to Part 2}
	
	While Part 1 presented the conceptual and philosophical foundation of the Fundamental Fractal-Geometric Field Theory (FFGFT) and introduced the central idea of **Time-Mass Duality** as well as the four fundamental manifestations of the constant \(\xi\), Part 2 now turns to the **technical heart** of the theory.
	
	This volume focuses on three closely interconnected tasks:
	
	1. The systematic mathematical derivation of the most important known natural constants and particle parameters starting from the single dimensionless number \(\xi \approx 1.33 \times 10^{-4}\)
	
	2. The presentation and discussion of the concrete formulas and derivation paths through which most of the 26+ free parameters of the Standard Model (and several cosmological quantities) can be shown to be derived
	
	3. The direct comparison of the T0/FFGFT expressions with the corresponding Standard Model values, together with a discussion of the achieved accuracy and the remaining open questions
	
	Part 2 is therefore significantly more technical and formula-rich than Part 1. It is primarily aimed at readers who want to know:
	
	• What exactly do the concrete derivation paths look like?  
	• Which assumptions are actually being made?  
	• How large are the deviations between theory and measurement at present?  
	• Which parameters can already be reproduced remarkably well?  
	• Where lie the greatest challenges and the still unresolved subtleties?
	
	The chapters of this part essentially follow a hierarchical order – from the most fundamental geometric and dimensionless ratios (Levels 1–3) through the coupling constants and fine structure (Levels 4–5) to mixing matrices (Level 6) and derived quantities such as the Weinberg angle or the strong CP phase (Level 7).
	
\textbf{Important note for readers:}

Most sections in this volume build directly on concepts introduced in Part 1, especially the four manifestations of the constant \(\xi\), the principle of fractal scaling, and the core idea of Time-Mass Duality. Familiarity with these foundations is therefore recommended.  

That said, many chapters are self-contained enough to be read selectively: readers interested only in the derivation of particular constants (such as the fine-structure constant, gravitational constant, neutrino mixing angles, or lepton mass ratios) can focus on the relevant sections without needing to read the entire preceding material.
	
	Formulas and derivations are deliberately presented in detail – even where agreement with experiment is not yet perfect. These very places are often the most insightful, as they most clearly reveal the boundaries and possible extensions of the theory.
	
	Welcome to the mathematical engine room of Time-Mass Duality – where the structure of known physics is step by step reconstructed from a single number.
