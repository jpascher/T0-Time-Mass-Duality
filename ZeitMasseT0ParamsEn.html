2 Introduction

This paper examines the connection between natural unit systems and dimensionless constants in the T0 model of time-mass duality theory. It is argued that the parameter \(β_{\text{T}}^{\text{SI}} ≈ 0.008\) in the temperature-redshift relation \(T(z) = T_0 (1+z)(1+β_{\text{T}}^{\text{SI}}\ln(1+z))\) can be set to \(β_{\text{T}}^{\text{nat}} = 1\) in natural units, analogous to Wien’s constant \(α_{\text{W}}\) . Additionally, the parameters \(κ\), \(α\) and \(β_{\text{T}}\) of the T0 model are derived in detail and linked to cosmological implications. For a further analysis of the consistency when simultaneously setting the fine structure constant \(α_{\text{EM}}= 1\) and the parameter \(β_{\text{T}}^{\text{nat}} = 1\), see .

1 Dimensionless Parameters in Fundamental Theories

1.1 Historical Development and Principles

Physics shows an evolution towards unit systems in which natural constants are set to 1:

  • Maxwell: \(c\) as a fundamental constant

  • Theory of Relativity: \(c = 1\)

  • Quantum Mechanics: \(\hbar = 1\)

  • Quantum Gravitation: \(G = 1\)

Dimensionless parameters should be simple (e.g., 1, \(π\)). \(β_{\text{T}}^{\text{SI}} ≈ 0.008\) suggests a non-optimal system. This historical progression toward simpler parameter values aligns with the principle that fundamental theories should have elegant mathematical formulations, as discussed in and further elaborated in the context of the T0 model in .

1.2 The Significance of the "Right" Natural Units

Complex values like \(β_{\text{T}}^{\text{SI}} ≈ 0.008\) suggest that the formulation is not fundamental. Historical examples:

  • \(c = 1\) in appropriate units

  • \(\hbar = 1\) in quantum units

  • \(G = 1\) in Planck units

The selection of appropriate natural units is not merely a mathematical convenience but reveals underlying physical principles, as emphasized in . The T0 model extends this philosophy to include the dimensionless parameter \(β_{\text{T}}\), suggesting that its natural value should be unity in an appropriately chosen unit system.

2 The Characteristic Length Scale \(r_0\)

2.1 Redefinition of \(r_0\) in Natural Units

The length scale \(r_0\) is defined as \(r_0 = ξ · l_P\), where \(ξ\) is a dimensionless constant and \(l_P = \sqrt{(\hbar G)/(c^3)}\) is the Planck length. In natural units (\(\hbar = c = G = 1\)), \(l_P = 1\), thus \(r_0 = ξ\).

From \(β_{\text{T}}^{\text{nat}} = 1\) and:

\[β_{\text{T}}^{\text{nat}} = (λ_h^2 v^2)/(16π^3 m_h^2 ξ){16π^3 m_h^2} · (1)/(ξ)\](1)
follows:
\[ξ = (λ_h^2 v^2)/(16π^3 m_h^2) ≈ 1.33 × 10^{-4}\](2)
\[r_0 ≈ (1)/(7519) · l_P\](3)

This derivation establishes \(r_0\) as a fundamental length scale of the T0 model, directly linked to the Higgs parameters and the Planck scale. The implications of this connection are further explored in and .

2.2 Physical Interpretation

\(r_0\) is the interaction length between \(T(x)\) and the Higgs field:

  • Correlation of fluctuations

  • Transition between quantum and classical gravitation

  • Coupling to the electroweak sector

This indicates a Planck-scale connection. As discussed in , this characteristic length plays a crucial role in the emergence of gravitation from the dynamics of the intrinsic time field \(T(x)\).

2.3 Conversion Between Natural Units and SI Units

\[\begin{aligned} r_{0,\text{SI}} &= ξ · l_{P,\text{SI}} \\ &= 1.33 × 10^{-4} · 1.616255e-35 m \\ &≈ 2.15e-39 m \end{aligned}\](4)
\[\begin{aligned} β_{\text{T}}^{\text{SI}} &= β_{\text{T}}^{\text{nat}} · \frac{ξ · l_{P,\text{SI}}}{r_{0,\text{SI}}} \\ &= 1 · \frac{ξ · l_{P,\text{SI}}}{r_{0,\text{SI}}} \\ &≈ 0.008 \end{aligned}\](5)

This conversion establishes the precise relationship between the natural units value \(β_{\text{T}}^{\text{nat}} = 1\) and the SI value \(β_{\text{T}}^{\text{SI}} ≈ 0.008\), clarifying that these are not contradictory but represent the same physical reality in different unit systems, similar to how the speed of light can be expressed as \(c = 1\) or \(c = 3e8 m/s\) (see ).

2.4 Consistency with the Cosmological Length Scale \(L_T\)

\[L_T ∼ \frac{M_{\text{Pl}}}{m_h^2 v} ≈ 6.3e27 m\](6)
\[(r_0)/(L_T) ∼ (λ_h^2 v^4)/(16π^3 M_{\text{Pl)}} ≈ 3.41 × 10^{-67}\](7)

This ratio is remarkable as it is of the order of \((m_e/M_{Pl})^2\), possibly indicating a deeper connection to the electron mass. The relationship between microscopic and cosmological scales in the T0 model points to a unified description of phenomena across vastly different length scales, as further explored in .

3 Parameter Derivations in the T0 Model

3.1 Derivation of \(κ\)

Theorem 1 (Derivation of \(κ\)). In natural units with dimension \([E]\):

\[κ^{\text{nat}} = β_{\text{T}}^{\text{nat}} · (yv)/(r_g^2)β_{\text{T}}^{\text{nat}} · (yv)/(r_g^2), \quad r_g = \sqrt{(M)/(a_0)}\](8)
In SI units:
\[κ_{\text{SI}} = β_{\text{T}}^{\text{SI}} (y v c^2)/(r_g^2) ≈ 4.8e-11 m/s²\](9)
where \(y\) is the Yukawa coupling, \(v\) the Higgs vacuum expectation value, \(M\) the mass, and \(a_0\) an acceleration scale.

This parameter \(κ\) is central to the modified gravitational potential in the T0 model and has been shown to account for galaxy rotation curves without requiring dark matter, as detailed in . Its dimensional consistency with \([E]\) in natural units highlights the role of energy as a fundamental quantity in the unified description.

3.2 Derivation of \(α\)

Theorem 2 (Derivation of \(α\)). In natural units with dimension \([E]\):

\[α = (λ_h^2 v)/(L_T)\](10)
In SI units:
\[α_{\text{SI}} = (λ_h^2 v c^2)/(L_T) ≈ 2.3e-18 /m\](11)
where \(λ_h\) is the Higgs self-coupling.

The parameter \(α\) describes the spatial variation of the intrinsic time field \(T(x)\) and plays a crucial role in the T0 model’s explanation of cosmic redshift without requiring universal expansion, as discussed in . Its connection to the Higgs parameters further emphasizes the unification of microscopic and cosmological phenomena in the T0 framework.

3.3 Derivation of \(β\): From Natural to SI Units

Theorem 3 (Derivation of \(β\)). In natural units with dimension \([1]\) (dimensionless): \(β_{\text{T}}^{\text{nat}} = 1\).

The primary formulation is:

\[β_{\text{T}}^{\text{nat}} = (λ_h^2 v^2)/(16π^3 m_h^2 ξ){16π^3 m_h^2 ξ}\](12)

With \(ξ = (λ_h^2 v^2)/(16π^3 m_h^2)\), we obtain \(β_{\text{T}}^{\text{nat}} = 1\).

In SI units:

\[β_{\text{T}}^{\text{SI}} = β_{\text{T}}^{\text{nat}} · \frac{ξ · l_{P,\text{SI}}}{r_{0,\text{SI}}} ≈ 0.008\](13)

The parameter \(β_{\text{T}}\) is a dimensionless coupling constant that characterizes the interaction between the intrinsic time field and other fields. Its natural value of unity in the appropriate unit system suggests a fundamental significance, similar to setting \(α_{\text{EM}}= 1\) for the fine structure constant as proposed in . The relationship between \(β_{\text{T}}^{\text{nat}} = 1\) and \(β_{\text{T}}^{\text{SI}} ≈ 0.008\) is explored in detail in .

4 Quantum Theoretical Determination of the Parameter \(β_{\text{T}}\)

The quantum field theoretical analysis of the T0 model yields a perturbative value for the dimensionless parameter \(β_{\text{T}}^{\text{SI}} ≈ 0.008\) in SI units, which is consistent with cosmological observations. This value was derived through a perturbative treatment of the interaction between the intrinsic time field \(T(x)\) and matter, with the fundamental time-mass duality \(m = (\hbar)/(T(x)c^2)\) as a starting point. In particular, it is shown that \(β_{\text{T}}^{\text{SI}}\) reflects the strength of the coupling between time field fluctuations and cosmic expansion, manifesting in a wavelength-dependent redshift \(z(λ) = z_0 \left(1 + β_{\text{T}}^{\text{SI}} \ln (λ)/(λ_0)\right)\) as well as in modified rotation curves of galaxies. A comprehensive presentation of this derivation, including experimental verifiability through cosmological measurements, can be found in , especially in the section "Experimental Tests and Predictions."

A deeper theoretical consideration reveals, however, that in natural units (\(\hbar = c = 1\)), the parameter \(β_{\text{T}}^{\text{nat}} = 1\) is equivalent. This equivalence arises from the scaling property of the time-mass duality, which enables a unified representation of physical quantities in natural units. In the T0 model, mass is defined as an inverse function of the time field, and the choice of natural units eliminates dimensionful constants such as \(\hbar\) and \(c\), giving \(β_{\text{T}}\) a universal meaning. In , section "Natural Units in the T0 Model," it is shown that this transition is not merely a mathematical simplification, but also reveals fundamental connections between time, mass, and gravitation. For example, the field equation

\[∇^2 T(x)= -κ ρ(\vec{x}) T(x)^2\](16)
where \(κ\) has the dimension \([E]\) and \(ρ\) has the dimension \([E^2]\) in natural units, leads to a direct connection between the mass density \(ρ(\vec{x})\) and the gradients of the time field that generate emergent gravitation.

The discrepancy between \(β_{\text{T}}^{\text{SI}} ≈ 0.008\) and \(β_{\text{T}}^{\text{nat}} = 1\) is thus not a contradiction, but an artifact of the chosen unit systems. In SI units, \(β_{\text{T}}\) is scaled by the specific values of \(\hbar\), \(c\), and other constants, while natural units eliminate this scaling and present \(β_{\text{T}}\) as a unified coupling constant. This duality of representation has far-reaching implications: While \(β_{\text{T}}^{\text{SI}}\) is directly linked to observable quantities such as cosmic acceleration and galaxy dynamics, \(β_{\text{T}}^{\text{nat}}\) provides a theoretical foundation for the unification of the T0 model with other physical theories, such as the Higgs mechanism or entropic gravitation, as further elaborated in . Future work could aim to refine the quantum theoretical derivation of \(β_{\text{T}}\) through non-perturbative methods to further substantiate the consistency between these two values.

10 Conclusions

Setting \(β_{\text{T}}^{\text{nat}} = 1\) in natural units of the T0 model represents a conceptually elegant and physically motivated simplification, analogous to setting \(c = 1\) in the theory of relativity or \(\hbar = 1\) in quantum mechanics. This simplification requires a specific interpretation of the characteristic length scale \(r_0\) as \(r_0 ≈ 1.33 × 10^{-4} · l_P\), which corresponds to a specific ratio to the Planck length.

The resulting discrepancy with current "measurements" can be understood as an indication that our interpretation of cosmological data may be too strongly influenced by the paradigmatic framework of the standard model. This opens the door for new perspectives and experimental tests that could distinguish between different cosmological models.

For practical application and comparison with experimental data, all results can be easily translated back into SI units. The conceptual elegance of a theory with simple dimensionless parameters (\(β_{\text{T}}^{\text{nat}} = 1\)) versus complex values (\(β_{\text{T}}^{\text{SI}} ≈ 0.008\)) supports a deeper investigation of this possibility, especially in the context of the time-mass duality theory, which already proposes fundamental reinterpretations of physical concepts as detailed in and .

In conclusion, the setting of \(β_{\text{T}}^{\text{nat}} = 1\) in the T0 model not only simplifies the mathematical formulation but also reveals deeper connections between seemingly disparate physical phenomena, from quantum field theory to cosmology. The resulting unified framework offers new perspectives on some of the most challenging problems in contemporary physics, such as the nature of dark matter and dark energy, as discussed throughout this work and the related papers cited herein.
